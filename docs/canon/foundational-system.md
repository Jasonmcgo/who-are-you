# Who Are You? Foundational System

## Core Thesis

Who Are You? is not a personality quiz, biography tool, political survey, or therapy app.

It is a structured self-discovery system designed to help a person distinguish:

- inherited assumptions
- stated beliefs
- lived behavior
- pressure-tested convictions
- internal tensions
- shared human constants

The system should not place users into a single box. It should resolve a person through a collage of structured cards, interpreted through lenses, with provenance and user correction.

## BlockVera Analogy

BlockVera:

Federal law + jurisdiction + CBA + policy + labor levels + holidays + worker card  
= unique client/worker setup

Who Are You?:

Formation + context + role + temperament + conviction + pressure + contradiction + agency + sacred values  
= self configuration

## Core Principle

The app should not tell the user who they are.

It should show the evidence, offer an interpretation, and ask:

“Is this true?”

## System Flow

Raw Inputs  
→ Cards  
→ Signals  
→ Interpretation Lenses  
→ Tensions  
→ Self Configuration  
→ Inner Constitution

## Core Cards

### 1. Formation Card

Where you came from.

Captures past shaping:
- upbringing
- family dynamics
- early authority model
- moral language
- economic or cultural environment
- early reinforcement

Formation is the soil, not the verdict.

The system should reveal inherited defaults without judging them.

### 2. Context Card

Where you are now.

Captures current external pressures:
- work environment
- current family obligations
- social surroundings
- economic reality
- time and energy constraints
- community environment

Context explains why people may not live out their ideals.

### 3. Role Card

What is expected of you.

Captures behavioral expectations:
- parent
- spouse
- leader
- employee
- creator
- caretaker
- citizen
- friend

Roles are not identity, but they shape behavior.

### 4. Temperament Card

How you are wired.

Inputs may include:
- MBTI
- Predictive Index
- Accumax
- direct behavioral questions

Known frameworks can accelerate the process, but should not define the person.

MBTI should be translated into cognitive process tendencies where useful.

### 5. Conviction Card

What you believe when values collide.

Captures:
- value hierarchy
- moral instincts
- institutional trust
- human nature assumptions
- responsibility model
- tradeoff tolerance
- freeform convictions

The system should force meaningful tradeoffs rather than allowing vague agreement.

### 6. Pressure Test Card

What survives when belief becomes expensive.

Captures:
- social pressure
- economic pressure
- reputation pressure
- authority pressure
- outcome pressure
- isolation scenarios
- belief stability over time

Conviction is the claim. Pressure is the proof.

### 7. Contradiction Card

Where the system detects tension, not failure.

Captures:
- belief vs behavior
- belief vs belief
- belief vs pressure
- formation vs conviction
- context vs identity
- structured answers vs freeform writing

Contradictions should be framed as tensions, not flaws.

### 8. Agency Card

What you actually do.

Captures:
- time allocation
- creative output
- habits
- avoidance patterns
- sacrifices
- commitments
- repeated choices

Agency grounds the system in lived reality.

### 9. Sacred Values Card

What must not be violated.

Captures:
- non-negotiables
- what the user would defend
- what the user would sacrifice for
- what they believe should be preserved

### 10. Bridge / Human Constants Layer

Identifies shared moral ground across different worldviews.

Examples:
- work
- love
- give
- protect children
- oppose exploitation
- preserve human dignity
- seek belonging
- desire meaning

The goal is not false agreement.

The goal is to show that people may arrive at similar moral instincts through very different frameworks.

## Interpretation Rule

Never say:

“You believe this because of your upbringing.”

Say:

“Your background may have influenced this pattern. Does that feel accurate?”

## Provenance Rule

Every interpretation should be traceable to source cards.

Example:

“This tension appears to come from:
- Conviction Card: Truth above loyalty
- Pressure Test Card: adapts under social cost
- Context Card: high relational responsibility”

## User Correction Rule

Every major interpretation must allow the user to:
- accept
- partially accept
- reject
- explain

The user remains the final authority on self-description.

## Product Output

The final human-readable output is the Inner Constitution.

It should include:
- what the user values
- what they believe
- what they protect
- what they fear
- where they are conflicted
- what appears inherited
- what appears chosen
- where they may share common ground with unlike people

## Tone

The tone should be:
- philosophical
- humane
- precise
- non-judgmental
- slightly unsettling when appropriate

Avoid:
- pop psychology
- political labeling
- therapy-speak
- moral scolding
- premature classification

## Foundational Product Statement

Who Are You? helps users recover themselves from biography, tribe, performance, fear, and imitation.

It does not give them a type.

It helps them build a living self-portrait.
