## Overall judgment

Your current concept is strongest when it is framed exactly as your working session frames it: not as diagnosis, not as a reductive type code, but as a structured self-portrait that organizes how a person interprets reality, prioritizes values, assigns agency, trusts sources, responds to stress, and orients growth. That product instinct is sound. The problem is not the ambition of the model; it is that some cards already sit on firm research foundations while others are still narrative hypotheses dressed in scientific clothing. ## Where the model is speculative but promising

The most promising move in your architecture is not any single card. It is the attempt to combine **values**, **agency**, **trust**, and **context** into one interpretable portrait. That is good synthesis. Research fields often study those domains separately; product frameworks become more useful when they help a user see how they interact. The second danger is **treating all eight cards as the same kind of thing**. They are not. Compass is mostly about motivational priorities. Gravity is about causal explanation and control beliefs. ## How to make the framework psychometrically cleaner

The first design decision is architectural. This should be developed as a **profile battery**, not a single scale. In psychometric terms, some cards are likely reflective constructs, some are formative composites, and some are state overlays. That means you should not produce one grand ŌĆ£Who Are You score,ŌĆØ and you should be cautious even about comparing card totals as though they sit on one common metric. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34view1Ņłü

The second decision is temporal. Separate the model into **trait-like**, **state-like**, and **contextual** layers. A scientifically cleaner version would treat Lens, Compass, Gravity, Trust, and perhaps Conviction as relatively durable profile dimensions; Weather and Fire as current overlays or contingencies; and Path as directional motive rather than a trait score. This aligns better with evidence that people display stable central tendencies yet also substantial within-person variability across situations. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn14view9Ņłéturn41view0Ņłéturn41view2Ņłü

The third decision is measurement format. For some cards, conventional Likert items are fine. For others, they are not enough. Compass and sacred-value content should use **forced-choice trade-offs** and short vignettes, because identity is clearer when a person must privilege one good over another. Modern forced-choice methodology now allows normative scoring while also helping with social-desirability bias. Gravity should use event-based attribution prompts. Fire should use scenario-based coping items. Trust should specify referents explicitly: experts, peers, institutions, close others, and transmitted knowledge. The fourth decision is validation strategy. ## Open questions and limits

The main unresolved issue is not whether the framework is interesting. It is whether you want it to be **more elegant** or **more measurable**. The present eight-card language is elegant. A more evidence-based backbone will be less mystical and more dimensional. That trade is worth making.

A second limit is that some relevant literatures were easier to access through abstracts, open reviews, and official measure repositories than through full journal texts. That affects how fine-grained some instrument comparisons can be. It does not materially change the central conclusions: keep the framework, narrow its claims, abandon Jungian functions as the measurement spine, distinguish traits from overlays, and root user-facing outputs in validated constructs plus conditional interpretation rather than fixed typology.
